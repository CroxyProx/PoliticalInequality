Definition and Root Causes

Political inequality refers to the unequal distribution of political participation, influence, and representation across diverse groups. In modern democracies, citizens have the same rights, but research shows that political power is often correlated with higher income, education, organizational strength, and access to the lawmakers of said country (Bartels, 2008; Busemeyer et al., 2024). This creates a gap between the principle of equal citizenship and the truth of the unequal distribution of political say. This is especially more prominent with groups more closely aligned with policy outcomes than lower-income citizens (Bartels, 2008; Schlozman, Brady, & Verba, 2012). Recent writings also show that political inequality is not isolated from economic inequality. Rather, both have their own ties together, in a way that strengthens the economic gap, because of the support of governmental infrastructure for the above side (Cagé, 2024; Mian, Straub, & Sufi, 2024).

Challenges Posed

The challenges created by political inequality are serious because unequal participation and representation weaken democratic responsiveness. Research in rich democracies says that people with fewer resources participate far less, while decision-makers tend to be more aligned with the preferences of the upper quartile, making representative democracies biased towards higher-status groups (Busemeyer et al., 2024). Studies of political representation in the United States similarly show that national policymakers are more responsive to affluent citizens rather than middle or lower class citizens, especially when their interests collide at opposite ends of the spectrum (Bartels, 2008; Gilens, 2012). This imbalance can reduce the trust in democratic institutions, therefore reducing democratic participation and making citizens feel that political institutions don't reflect their interests. This affects democratic legitimacy (Schlozman et al., 2012; Pontusson & Rueda, 2010).

Relevance

Political inequality is even more relevant today because it has been linked to democratic erosion and growing public disagreement. International research databases find that unequal income distribution is associated with a higher risk of democratic backsliding and increased polarization, which often leads to the connection between inequality and institutional decline (Mian et al., 2024). At the same time, writings on political inequality show that in recent decades, inequalities in participation, donations, and representation have been widening, and that gap has only increased, which makes the problem visible and more difficult to reverse (Cagé, 2024). For these reasons, political inequality is not only a fairness issue but also a structural threat to democratic stability and civil trust in the future of a modern democratic society.
